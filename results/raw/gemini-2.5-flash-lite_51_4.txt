**Understanding the Condition**

We need 3 distinct classes to meet *strictly inside* the square. This means that at some internal point, there must be three different labels present. The boundaries of these classes are continuous curves.

**Key Insight: Corner Labels Dictate Internal Regions**

The labels at the corners are the only information we have. If we have 3 distinct classes, they must somehow extend from the corners to create regions inside the square. The simplest way for three distinct classes to meet *inside* is if the three distinct corner labels are adjacent to each other in a way that forces their boundaries to intersect internally.

**Corner Order:** Top-left, bottom-right, bottom-left, top-right.

Let's represent the corner labels as (TL, BR, BL, TR).

**Conditions for Internal Intersection of 3 Classes:**

For 3 distinct classes to meet internally, we need a configuration where the three distinct labels are "surrounding" an internal region. This will happen if and only if at least three of the corners have distinct labels.

Consider the labels at the corners. If we have only 1 or 2 distinct labels, it's impossible for 3 distinct classes to meet internally. For example, if all corners are '0', only one class exists. If we have '0' and '1', we can have a boundary separating two classes, but not three meeting internally.

So, we *must* have at least 3 distinct labels among the 4 corners.

Now, let's think about how these 3 distinct labels could be arranged. If we have 3 distinct labels, say A, B, and C, they must occupy at least 3 corners. The fourth corner can be one of these 3.
